This story started from my family’s Christmas Eve tradition. My great-grandfather, Gyu Shin Lee, passed away on Christmas Eve and my family gathers at home every Christmas Eve to share memories, eulogy, and his legacy through a Christian memorial service. My grandfather, Jhong Soo Lee, brings his parents' portrait, my great-grandfather’s hand-calligraphed Lee family history book, Korean traditional genealogy, Jokbo, and his diary. There is an old Korean proverb, ‘A tiger dies and leaves his skin; a man dies and leaves his name.’ These objects endured during the turbulent history and, as the fourth generation, I feel responsible to sustain his legacy and transferring family values to later history through printed and digital media with a new interpretation of family genealogy.

The dialogue in the book was adapted from many years of recordings of the memorial service. The story preserves narratives of an intergenerational Korean family and brings special bonds between the generations, and gives a sense of belonging. Looking at my great-grandfather's legacy, I ask myself – what do family and connection mean to me in this modern society and how can I continue to archive, persevere, interpret and recreate the legacy to set my identity?
